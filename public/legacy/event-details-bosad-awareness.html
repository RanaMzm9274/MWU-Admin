About the event

The College of Medicine and Health Sciences at Madda Walabu University, Goba Referral Hospital, in partnership with the national BOne Setting Associated Disability (BOSAD) research team, held a one-day awareness and capacity-building dialogue for traditional bone setters drawn from several woredas.

The main objective was to reduce preventable complications related to bone and joint injuries by strengthening practical cooperation between traditional practitioners and modern health professionals.

  • Improving early recognition and safer first response for bone and joint injuries.
  • Avoiding harmful practices such as overly tight binding and repeated deep massage of injured limbs.
  • Promoting timely referral and communication with nearby health facilities.
  • Supporting regular capacity-building for traditional practitioners at community level.

Dr Lopiso Hailemariam, Orthopaedic Specialist at MWU Goba Referral Hospital, explained that the training is essential for reducing complex complications and for guiding how traditional practitioners can work in coordination with modern care during injury emergencies.

Dr Zinaye Wude, Orthopaedics and Sports Injury Specialist, also highlighted that tightly binding injured parts and repeated massage without clinical consultation can worsen damage, including severe tissue and circulation complications. He stressed that stronger referral practice and teamwork can reduce amputation and death.

The session trained more than 50 traditional practitioners from Robe Town, Goba, Dinsho, Agarfa, and Sinana woredas. Participants noted that some long-used methods, including forceful massage and tight splinting, can cause serious harm, and they committed to consulting and collaborating with local health professionals going forward.
