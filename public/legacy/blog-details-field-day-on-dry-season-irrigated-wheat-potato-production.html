Madda Walabu University organized a field visit to the dry-season irrigated wheat production site in Sheneka Kebele, Agarfa District, under the theme Advancing Irrigated Wheat and Potato Production for a More Resilient Future.

The program highlighted practical progress in dry-season production, groundwater irrigation, and collaborative agricultural innovation designed to improve food security, strengthen climate resilience, and support local farmers.

Key Information

  • Theme: Advancing Irrigated Wheat and Potato Production for a More Resilient Future
  • Location: Sheneka Kebele, Agarfa District
  • Partners: Madda Walabu University, Grow Further (USA), and Agarfa District Agriculture Office
  • Focus: Dry-season wheat and potato production, groundwater irrigation, and farmer-centered technology transfer

Program Highlights

  • University management, faculty members, students, farmers, district officials, and agricultural experts joined the visit
  • Participants observed wheat cultivation supported by groundwater irrigation during the dry season
  • Technical support and improved seed varieties were demonstrated for local farmers
  • The visit also formed part of the Farmers Research Conference activities held in the previous week

The field visit demonstrated how practical research, farmer collaboration, and water-efficient irrigation can strengthen food security and climate resilience.

MWU Field Day Program

Procedure and Observations

  • University researchers guided participants through wheat and potato demonstration fields
  • The team reviewed the use of groundwater extracted from depths of 12 to 15 meters
  • Farmers shared experiences on dry-season production, crop rotation, and improved productivity
  • Stakeholders discussed opportunities for expanding irrigated production in other locations

Research and Technical Contribution

The project principal investigator, Jemal Abdulkerim, Assistant Professor and researcher at Madda Walabu University, explained that the initiative began two years ago. Since then, the team has evaluated and adapted approximately 24 wheat varieties, including Kulmisa, Boru, Gutu, Baro, Bekelcha, Mandoyu, Melka, Sheremo, Abay, and Derba.

The field testing process enabled farmers and experts to identify the varieties that performed best under local soil and water conditions. The work supports sustainable wheat and potato production, improved crop productivity, and more efficient use of irrigation water resources.

Important Notes

  • The initiative is implemented through a collaborative partnership with Grow Further (USA) and the Agarfa District Agriculture Office
  • The university leadership reaffirmed continued technical and institutional support for the project
  • Participants emphasized that stronger partnerships are essential for expanding irrigated wheat production and improving livelihoods
  • The program concluded with a shared commitment to long-term food security and climate resilience

Official Confirmation

The Head of the Agarfa District Agriculture Office, the Deputy District Administrator, university leadership, researchers, and participating farmers all expressed satisfaction with the visible progress achieved through the project. They encouraged continued joint work to strengthen agricultural productivity and community resilience.
